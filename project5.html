Solar Shading and Visual Comfort

Year: 2022 Type: Project Work Collaborators: KieranTimberlake Tools: Ladybug & Honeybee Tools

This study addresses potential visual discomfort in a single-family residence in Pennsylvania with extensive south-facing glazing. The challenge was identifying when and where glare occurs throughout the year, then recommending targeted shading strategies that preserve daylight and views while eliminating the problematic glaring sun.
Approach: Sun path diagrams established the seasonal range of solar angles. Hourly direct sun hours were mapped across the living and dining spaces for both summer and winter conditions. Point-in-time illuminance identified specific problematic moments: winter morning sun creating glare on the TV viewing area, low-angle winter midday sun hitting the dining table and kitchen island for 2-3 hours, and early summer morning sun striking the kitchen sink and counter.
Overhang effectiveness was tested at two depths. A 3'-0" overhang blocked summer midday sun but allowed afternoon penetration, while a 6'-0" overhang provided more comprehensive shading and reduced cooling loads. However, neither fully eliminated winter glare from the low-angle sun.
Key Findings: Recommended strategies included roof overhangs for peak summer shading, roller shades at select locations for year-round glare control, opacity treatments near the kitchen sink to block early morning direct sun, and courtyard trees positioned to shade south-facing glass in summer while limiting winter glare. This balanced passive solar design principles with occupant comfort across daily and seasonal cycles.
