Envelope & Thermal Parameters

Year: 2024 Type: Project Work Collaborators: Ryan Welch, Adam Loughry Tools: Ladybug & Honeybee Tools, Microsoft Excel, Design Explorer

This study examines retrofit strategies for Stuart Weitzman Hall, a historic brick building at the University of Pennsylvania. The challenge was determining which envelope improvements (wall insulation, roof insulation, or window replacement) would most effectively reduce energy loads while respecting the building's historic character.
Approach: The existing envelope was documented through archival blueprints, revealing a 17-inch brick wall with 2-inch finish and an R-value between 1.7 and 6.8. Key uncertainties included the masonry's susceptibility to freeze-thaw damage, environmental concerns around spray foam insulation, and viable alternatives for sensitive historic fabric. Strategies ranged from aggressive to more moderate approaches. Each combination of wall R-value, roof R-value, and window U-value was evaluated for peak heating and cooling loads reductions.
The analysis extended to the proposed addition, examining how window performance affects operational carbon emissions over the building's lifetime. By varying both U-values and solar heat gain coefficients, the study traced how glazing decisions translate to emissions from heating and cooling systems which was quantified in tons of CO2 equivalent to forest carbon sequestration.
Key Findings: Roof insulation yielded the highest return, reducing heating EUI by 73%. A moderate strategy combining roof, wall, and window improvements achieved 63% heating reduction. For the addition, window U-value significantly impacted emissions, while SHGC showed minimal influence, a 3% EUI change corresponded to variations of 1,500 tons CO2, roughly equal to carbon sequestered by 1,505 acres of US forest annually.

Envelope & Natural Ventilation

Year: 2024 Type: Project Work Collaborators: Kit Elsworth Tools: Ladybug & Honeybee Tools

This study investigates whether operable windows can improve thermal comfort in student residence hall lounges in Berkeley, California. The challenge was determining if natural ventilation could reduce hot indoor hours without relying solely on mechanical cooling systems.

Approach: Three lounges with different orientations (Northwest, Northeast, and South) were modeled to assess impact of operable windows (natural ventilation) on indoor air temperatures. The adaptive thermal comfort model established temperature-based control strategies reflecting the range where students could comfortably operate windows themselves.
Energy models examined energy load balances across heating, cooling, solar gains, and natural ventilation throughout representative days in January, April, and September. Annual simulations tracked both the frequency of above 81°F conditions and the duration of window operability, showing how orientation and solar exposure can affect natural ventilation potential. Key Findings: Natural ventilation dramatically reduced above 81°F conditions. The Northwest lounge saw hours above 81°F drop from 3,494 to 3,027 (13%) annually. The Northeast lounge achieved a 79% reduction, while the South lounge reduced overheated hours by 35%. When the upper temperature threshold was reduced to 78°F, reductions ranged from 22% to 28%, demonstrating that even modest expansions of the comfort range can significantly improve natural ventilation potential. The study showed that orientation with respect to solar exposure creates vastly different opportunities for passive cooling.
